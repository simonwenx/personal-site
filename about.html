Let's backtrack to the game I believed piqued my interest for coding, or at least problem solving. This would take us back to 2017, around the time I was in 5th grade. "Growtopia". To give some context, this game would essentially mimick concepts similar to real-world entrepreneurship. It would teach young kids about trust, cooperation, and growing capital. A reliable but inefficient way to gain capital in this game was called "farming". Don't overthink about what this term could mean, because it literally meant to farm crops and blocks. It was to harvest trees and plant seeds. Although this process would guarantee risk-free returns in capital, this often took too long and many would come to realize it's resemblence to a 9-5 job. Of course there were other ways to grow capital in this game, like running a business, or even reselling products, but nothing had risk-free returns like farming. Despite the fact that automatic farms already existed in the real world, the process had yet to be replicated in the game. This desire for more capital whilst saving time would embark an expedition to produce an automatic farm. At the ripe age of 10 I was introduced to high-levels of problem solving and this would carry on with me for the rest of my life. Being "rich" in this game felt like true freedom and I've lived under that consensus my entire life, to have money was to have freedom. Learning how to make money in this game sparked a deep passion for persistent problem solving and innovation.
